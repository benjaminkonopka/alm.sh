## The blue link is not dead. But it is no longer the main event.

For twenty years, digital marketing orbited around one question: where do you rank on Google? The answer shaped content strategy, site architecture, and entire agency business models. That question still matters. But a second one has joined it — and for a growing share of queries, it matters more: **does a language model cite you?**

When someone asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini a question about your industry, your product, or your category, the answer either references your content or it does not. There is no position two. There is no fold. You are either in the synthesized answer or you are invisible.

This is Generative Engine Optimization — GEO. And it is not SEO with a new acronym.

## What changed

Search engines ranked pages. Language models synthesize answers. The difference sounds small but it changes everything about what you optimize for.

**Authority signals are different.** Google used backlinks and domain authority. LLMs weigh factual consistency, citation density, and how often your content appears as a source across the training corpus and retrieval index. A well-linked blog post and a well-structured whitepaper are not equivalent anymore.

**Structure beats length.** A 3,000-word SEO article with keyword density tricks is noise to a language model. A clearly structured page with explicit claims, definitions, and data points is a citable source. LLMs extract fragments, not full pages.

**Freshness works differently.** Google's freshness signals rewarded frequent updates. LLM retrieval systems look for the most authoritative source at query time, which may be a well-maintained reference page that hasn't changed in six months.

## What to actually do

**Make your claims explicit.** If your page says your product reduces churn by 30%, that sentence should be a standalone, clearly attributed statement — not buried in the third paragraph of a case study narrative. LLMs extract discrete facts.

**Publish structured data aggressively.** JSON-LD, FAQ schema, how-to markup. Retrieval systems lean on structured data to identify citable content. If your content is not structured, it is harder to cite.

**Own your entity.** Make sure your company, product, and people have consistent, well-sourced descriptions across Wikipedia, Crunchbase, LinkedIn, and your own site. LLMs build entity representations from multiple sources — inconsistency is ambiguity, and ambiguity means you get left out.

**Monitor LLM citations, not just SERP rankings.** Tools like Otterly, GEOspy, and Profound are emerging to track whether and how models reference your content. If you are only watching Google Search Console, you are flying half-blind.

**Don't abandon SEO.** Google still drives the majority of web traffic. GEO is additive, not a replacement. The smart play is to optimize content for both — which mostly means writing content that is genuinely useful rather than gaming any particular algorithm.

## The agency implication

Agencies that sell SEO as a standalone service are about to have a hard conversation with their clients. The ones that reframe their offering as "discoverability" — across search, LLM citations, social, and voice — will own the next five years. The playbook is new. The goal is the same: be the answer, wherever the question is asked.
